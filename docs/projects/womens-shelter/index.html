Designing for Safety and Empowerment

This project was developed as a form of design activism in support of the Uppsala Women's Shelter. Our team sought to create digital experiences that protect victims of domestic and digital abuse while empowering them with discreet tools for help and information.

Drawing inspiration from user-centered design and safety-first thinking, we developed a redesign concept for the shelter's website, including an improved emergency exit button and a secure, temporary chat system. Additionally, we explored using Instagram ads to reach vulnerable groups through subtle, visually engaging campaigns.

Challenge

Victims of domestic abuse often face digital surveillance and lack safe, anonymous ways to seek help. The challenge was to design a discreet, mobile-friendly user experience that can reach younger audiences (especially Gen Z) and protect users in high-risk situations.

Project Overview

Timeline: 3 weeks
Tools: Figma, Canva, Notion
Role: UX Designer, Researcher

User Research & Insights

Research included interviews, web analysis, and feedback sessions with peers and instructors. Victims often delete their browsing history, use fake businesses to seek help, or rely on visual cues. Users responded positively to discreet designs and suggested multi-language support and chat anonymity features. We also investigated ad targeting strategies to ensure campaigns reach vulnerable groups via Instagram stories.

Design Concepts

We developed two key concepts:

  • Website Enhancements: A redesigned homepage with a more prominent exit button, a disappearing chat system, and a safety disclaimer on load.
  • Instagram Story Ads: A Valentine’s-themed campaign with soft, emotionally resonant imagery that avoids triggering language. Slide 1 grabs attention; slide 2 reveals emotional abuse signs.

Feedback & Refinement

Peer and instructor feedback emphasized the need for originality and clear focus. Based on that, we refined the ads to avoid explicit references and created a fake-business chat interface prototype. We also explored algorithm-based targeting and multilingual options for ads, especially for women with foreign backgrounds or substance abuse vulnerabilities.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates how design activism can be used to create digital experiences that prioritize safety, discretion, and social impact. By merging UX design principles with targeted outreach, we created a prototype that not only protects victims but also connects them to help in a subtle, respectful, and empowering way.
